<a title=\"Aerospace manufacturer\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer\">spacecraft manufacturer</a>, <a title=\"Space launch\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_launch\">launcher</a>, and <a class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Telecommunication\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunication\">satellite communications</a> company headquartered in <a title=\"Hawthorne, California\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawthorne,_California\">Hawthorne, California</a>. The company was founded in 2002 by <a title=\"Elon Musk\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elon_Musk\">Elon Musk</a> with the goal of reducing space transportation costs and to <a title=\"Colonization of Mars\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonization_of_Mars\">colonize Mars</a>. The company manufactures the <a title=\"Falcon 9\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9\">Falcon 9</a>, <a title=\"Falcon Heavy\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_Heavy\">Falcon Heavy</a> and <a title=\"SpaceX Starship\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Starship\">Starship</a> heavy-lift launch vehicles, the <a title=\"SpaceX Dragon 2\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon_2\">Cargo Dragon</a> and <a class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Crew Dragon\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crew_Dragon\">Crew Dragon</a> spacecrafts, the <a title=\"Starlink\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starlink\">Starlink</a> mega-constellation satellite and rocket engines.</p>\r\n<p>SpaceX offers commercial satellite-based <a class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Internet service\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_service\">internet service</a> via its <a title=\"Constellation\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation\">constellation</a> of Starlink satellites, which became the largest-ever satellite constellation in January 2020 and as of December 2022 comprised more than 3,300 <a title=\"Small satellite\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_satellite\">small satellites</a> in orbit.<sup id=\"cite_ref-sn2676101_7-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-sn2676101-7\">[7]</a></sup></p>\r\n<p>The company is also developing <a title=\"SpaceX Starship\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Starship\">Starship</a>, a privately funded, <a class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Fully-reusable orbital launch vehicle\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fully-reusable_orbital_launch_vehicle\">fully reusable</a>, <a title=\"Super heavy-lift launch vehicle\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_heavy-lift_launch_vehicle\">super heavy-lift launch system</a> for <a title=\"Interplanetary spaceflight\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_spaceflight\">interplanetary</a> and <a title=\"Orbital spaceflight\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight\">orbital spaceflight</a>. On its failed <a title=\"SpaceX Starship orbital test flight\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Starship_orbital_test_flight\">first flight in April 2023</a>, it became the largest and most powerful rocket ever flown. Starship is intended to become SpaceX's primary orbital vehicle, supplanting the existing Falcon 9, Falcon Heavy, and Dragon fleet.</p>\r\n<p>SpaceX is the first private company to develop a <a title=\"Liquid-propellant rocket\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket\">liquid-propellant rocket</a> that has reached orbit; to launch, orbit, and recover a spacecraft; to send a spacecraft to the <a title=\"International Space Station\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station\">International Space Station</a>; and to send astronauts to the International Space Station. It is also the first organization of any type to achieve a <a title=\"VTVL\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VTVL\">vertical propulsive landing</a> of an orbital rocket booster and the first to reuse such a booster. SpaceX's Falcon 9 rockets have landed and reflown <a title=\"List of Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy launches\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Falcon_9_and_Falcon_Heavy_launches\">more than developed three launch vehicles. The small-lift <a title=\"Falcon 1\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_1\">Falcon 1</a> was the first launch vehicle developed and was retired in 2009. The medium-lift <a title=\"Falcon 9\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9\">Falcon 9</a> and the heavy-lift <a title=\"Falcon Heavy\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_Heavy\">Falcon Heavy</a> are both operational. The <a title=\"Falcon 1\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_1\">Falcon 1</a> was a small rocket capable of placing several hundred kilograms into <a title=\"Low Earth orbit\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit\">low Earth orbit</a>. It launched five times between 2006 and 2009, of which 2 were successful.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Falcon9_overview_129-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-Falcon9_overview-129\">[128]</a></sup> The Falcon 1 was the first privately funded, liquid-fueled rocket to reach orbit.<sup id=\"cite_ref-nasa2008_110-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-nasa2008-110\">[110]</a></sup></p>\r\n<p><a title=\"Falcon 9\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9\">Falcon 9</a> is a <a title=\"Medium-lift launch vehicle\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium-lift_launch_vehicle\">medium-lift launch vehicle</a> capable of delivering up to 22,800 kilograms (50,265<span class=\"nowrap\">&nbsp;</span>lb) to orbit, competing with the <a title=\"Delta IV\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta_IV\">Delta IV</a> and the <a title=\"Atlas V\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_V\">Atlas V</a> rockets, as well as other launch providers around the world. It has nine <a title=\"SpaceX Merlin\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Merlin\">Merlin</a> engines in its first stage. The <a title=\"Falcon 9 v1.0\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9_v1.0\">Falcon 9 v1.0</a> rocket successfully reached orbit on its first attempt on 4 June 2010. Its third flight, <a title=\"SpaceX COTS Demo Flight 2\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_COTS_Demo_Flight_2\">COTS Demo Flight 2</a>, launched on 22 May 2012, and launched the first <a title=\"Private spaceflight\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_spaceflight\">commercial</a> spacecraft to reach and dock with the International Space Station (ISS).<sup id=\"cite_ref-cbcnews_51-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-cbcnews-51\">[51]</a></sup> The vehicle was upgraded to <a title=\"Falcon 9 v1.1\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9_v1.1\">Falcon 9 v1.1</a> in 2013, <a title=\"Falcon 9 Full Thrust\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9_Full_Thrust\">Falcon 9 Full Thrust</a> in 2015, and finally to <a title=\"Falcon 9 Block 5\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9_Block_5\">Falcon 9 Block 5</a> in 2018. The first stage of Falcon 9 is designed to retropropulsively land, be recovered, and reflown.<sup id=\"cite_ref-130\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-130\">[129]</a></sup></p>\r\n<p>The <a title=\"Falcon Heavy\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_Heavy\">Falcon Heavy</a> is a <a title=\"Heavy-lift launch vehicle\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy-lift_launch_vehicle\">heavy-lift launch vehicle</a> capable of delivering up to 63,800<span class=\"nowrap\">&nbsp;</span>kg (140,700<span class=\"nowrap\">&nbsp;</span>lb) to <a title=\"Low Earth orbit\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit\">Low Earth orbit</a> (LEO) or 26,700<span class=\"nowrap\">&nbsp;</span>kg (58,900<span class=\"nowrap\">&nbsp;</span>lb) to <a class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Geosynchronous transfer orbit\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_transfer_orbit\">geosynchronous transfer orbit</a> (GTO). The NASA contracts started as part of the Commercial Crew Development (CCDev) program, aimed at developing commercially operated spacecraft capable of delivering astronauts to the ISS. The first contract was awarded to SpaceX in 2011,<sup id=\"cite_ref-Space_Abort_System_20111028_229-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-Space_Abort_System_20111028-229\">[228]</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-aw20110422_230-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-aw20110422-230\">[229]</a></sup> followed by another in 2012 to continue development and testing of its <a title=\"SpaceX Dragon 2\" href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon_2\">Dragon 2</a> spacecraft.<sup id=\"cite_ref-August2012_231-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-August2012-231\">[230]</a></sup></p>\r\n<p>In September 2014, NASA chose SpaceX and Boeing as the two companies that would be funded to develop systems to transport U.S. crews to and from the ISS.<sup id=\"cite_ref-September2014_232-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX#cite_note-September2014-232\">[231]</a></sup> SpaceX won $2.6<span class=\"nowrap\">&nbsp;</span>billion to complete and certify Dragon 2 by 2017.
